Cochinita Pibil

Cochinita pibil is one of the most iconic dishes of the Yucatán Peninsula, and it represents a fusion of Mayan culinary tradition with ingredients introduced after Spanish colonization. At its core, cochinita pibil is pork slowly cooked in a deeply flavored marinade made from achiote and sour orange, a combination that produces a distinctive red color, a citrusy aroma, and an earthy, slightly sweet taste.

The word “pibil” comes from the Mayan technique pib, meaning “to cook underground.” Traditionally, the marinated pork was wrapped in banana leaves and placed inside a stone-lined earth oven where it cooked for hours with residual heat from buried embers. This low and slow technique breaks down the meat’s fibers and melts its fat, leaving the pork incredibly tender — so tender that it practically falls apart on its own.

Today, most people don’t cook cochinita in an underground oven, but the same effect is achieved by slow-roasting in an oven, pressure cooker, or slow cooker, often wrapped in banana leaves to retain moisture and add a subtle herbal aroma.

Cochinita pibil is commonly served in tacos, panuchos, salbutes, tortas, or as a main dish accompanied by rice or beans. It’s a celebration food, a comfort dish, and one of the clearest examples of how Mexican regional cuisine blends history, technique, and flavor into something both simple and extraordinary.

Ingredients

For the meat

  • 1.5 kg pork shoulder or pork leg
  • 100 g achiote paste
  • 1 cup sour orange juice (If you can’t find it: ½ cup orange juice + ½ cup lime juice)
  • 4 garlic cloves, crushed
  • 1 white onion, sliced
  • 2 teaspoons salt
  • 1 teaspoon black pepper
  • 1 teaspoon oregano
  • 1 banana leaf (optional but authentic)
  • Optional aromatics: cloves, allspice, bay leaves

For pickled onions

  • 1–2 red onions, thinly sliced
  • ½ cup lime juice
  • ¼ cup white vinegar
  • Salt
  • Habanero slices

Steps

Make the marinade

  1. In a blender, mix:
    • achiote paste
    • sour orange juice
    • garlic
    • salt
    • salt
    • salt
  2. Blend until smooth

The result should be a bright red-orange marinade.

Prepare the meat

  1. Cut the pork into large chunks (fist-sized).
  2. Place in a large bowl or baking dish.
  3. Pour the marinade over the meat and coat well.
  4. Add sliced onion on top.
  5. Cover and refrigerate minimum 4 hours — ideal is overnight.

Cook the cochinita

Option A — Oven (easiest)

  1. Preheat oven to 160°C (320°F).
  2. Line a baking tray or pot with banana leaf (optional).
  3. Place the marinated pork with all its juice.
  4. Cover tightly with foil or lid.
  5. Cook 3.5 to 4 hours, until the meat falls apart easily.

Option B — Slow cooker

  1. Low: 6–8 hours
  2. High: 4–5 hours

Make the pickled onions

  1. Mix red onion, lime juice, vinegar, and salt.
  2. Let sit at least 30 minutes.
  3. Add habanero if you want real Yucatecan flavor.

Shred and serve

  1. Shred the pork with forks — it should be extremely tender.
  2. Mix with some of the cooking liquid.
  3. Serve with:
    • corn tortillas
    • pickled onions
    • habanero salsa (optional)
